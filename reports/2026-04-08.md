### 1. [Understanding Educators' Perceptions of AI-generated Non-consensual Intimate Imagery](http://arxiv.org/abs/2604.06131v1)
**Authors:** Tongxin Li, Katelyn M Reyes, Liezeil Jimenez, Katie S Nam, Donghee Yvette Wohn | **Source:** Arxiv | **Date:** 2026-04-07 | **Type:** research paper | **Relevance:** 50%

**About the authors:** All authors are affiliated with the New Jersey Institute of Technology (NJIT). The senior/corresponding author, Donghee Yvette Wohn, is an associate professor and director of the Social X Lab at NJIT, with an h-index of 27 and over 11,000 citations on Google Scholar. Her research focuses on HCI, social media, content moderation, and online harassment. Tongxin (Lily) Li is a first-year/early-stage Ph.D. student at NJIT working in the Social X Lab under Dr. Wohn, focusing on human-AI interaction and digital media. Katelyn Reyes appears to be an undergraduate/graduate student at NJIT involved in the Social Interaction Lab. The other co-authors (Liezeil Jimenez, Katie S Nam) appear to be students in the same lab.

**Reliability:** MEDIUM confidence. The paper is accepted to CHI 2026 (a premier, peer-reviewed HCI venue), and the senior author (Wohn) has a strong, established research track record in HCI and social media. However, the first author is an early-career PhD student, the sample size is relatively small (n=20), and the findings, while valid, largely confirm what larger quantitative studies have already reported. The qualitative methodology is appropriate but limits generalizability. No red flags regarding data fabrication or questionable practices; the study addresses a genuine gap but with modest novelty.

**Overview:** This is a qualitative research paper (preprint on arXiv, accepted to CHI 2026) investigating U.S. educators' perceptions, attitudes, experiences, and practices related to AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery (AIG-NCII) in middle and high school settings. The central problem is understanding the gap between the emerging threat of AIG-NCII in schools and the actual supports, training, and policies available to educators to address it.

**Main goal:** The authors aim to identify what concrete supports schools need to effectively address AIG-NCII by interviewing 20 U.S. educators about their attitudes, experiences, and current practices, ultimately contributing to educational tool design, curriculum development, and policy-making around this emerging issue.

**Key findings:**
- Finding 1: Educators expressed dual vulnerability concerns — they worried both about the moral decline among students exposed to AIG-NCII technology and about themselves potentially becoming victims of AI-generated imagery by students.
- Finding 2: Existing school practices for addressing AIG-NCII are severely limited; educators lack both specialized training and systematic institutional policies, leaving them ill-equipped to respond to incidents.
- Finding 3: Key implementation barriers include a lack of resources, unclear legal boundaries around AIG-NCII (especially involving minors), and limited educator knowledge of AI technology, making effective prevention and response difficult.

**Methodology:** Semi-structured qualitative interview study with 20 educators in the United States (likely middle and high school teachers and administrators). Interviews explored educators' attitudes toward AIG-NCII, their direct or indirect experiences with such incidents, and current institutional practices. Analysis likely employed thematic coding or similar qualitative analysis methods to identify key themes across the interview data. The sample size of 20 is typical for exploratory qualitative HCI research.

**What's novel:** This paper is among the first to specifically examine AIG-NCII from the educator perspective through qualitative interviews, whereas prior work (e.g., CDT's 2024 survey, Brigham et al. 2024) has focused on quantitative prevalence data or general public perceptions. The focus on educators as both potential responders and potential victims is a novel dual framing. The paper also explicitly contributes to interactive educational tool design and curriculum recommendations, connecting HCI design implications to the problem.

**Limitations:** The sample of 20 U.S. educators, while acceptable for qualitative research, limits generalizability across different school systems, regions, or countries. The study likely skews toward educators who are already aware of or concerned about AIG-NCII, introducing possible self-selection bias. The paper does not include student or parent perspectives, which would provide a more complete picture. The rapidly evolving legal landscape (e.g., the TAKE IT DOWN Act signed in May 2025) may have already changed some of the policy gaps identified. As a qualitative study, it cannot quantify the prevalence or severity of AIG-NCII in schools.

**Implications:** The paper has direct practical implications for school policy-makers needing to develop AIG-NCII response frameworks, curriculum designers creating AI literacy and digital citizenship programs, and HCI researchers designing interactive educational tools. The finding that educators feel vulnerable themselves (not just concerned for students) adds urgency to institutional protections. The call for multi-stakeholder strategies aligns with emerging policy recommendations from organizations like CDT and ATIXA. For AI safety broadly, it highlights how generative AI misuse creates concrete, real-world harms in educational settings that demand sociotechnical rather than purely technical interventions.

**Critical assessment:** The paper addresses a timely and important topic with growing real-world urgency, as evidenced by the federal TAKE IT DOWN Act and CDT's large-scale surveys confirming NCII prevalence in schools. The qualitative methodology is appropriate for an exploratory study in an under-researched area. However, n=20 is on the smaller side even for qualitative work, and the findings — that schools lack resources, training, and policies — are somewhat predictable and consistent with prior survey data (e.g., CDT found 60% of teachers report their schools haven't shared NCII policies). The contributions to HCI/design are stated but the paper would be stronger with concrete design proposals or prototypes. The paper fills a genuine gap by centering educator voices qualitatively, but its findings may have limited novelty beyond confirming what quantitative surveys have already suggested. The acceptance to CHI 2026 (a top-tier HCI venue) lends credibility, though the paper sits at the intersection of HCI and education/policy rather than at the core of either field.

**Key terms:**
- **AIG-NCII:** AI-Generated Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery — sexually explicit or intimate images of real people created using AI tools (e.g., 'nudify' apps or deepfake generators) without the depicted person's consent.
- **NCII:** Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery — any intimate or sexually explicit images shared without the depicted person's consent, encompassing both authentic (real) images and AI-generated/synthetic ones.
- **Image-Based Sexual Abuse (IBSA):** A broader term encompassing all forms of non-consensual creation, distribution, or threat of distribution of intimate images, including 'revenge porn', upskirting, deepfake pornography, and sextortion.
- **Nudify apps:** AI-powered applications that take a clothed photo of a person and generate a realistic-looking but fake nude image, enabling even unskilled users to create deepfake NCII rapidly and at scale.
- **Multi-stakeholder strategy:** An approach to addressing AIG-NCII that involves coordinated action across educators, administrators, parents, students, policymakers, technology platforms, and law enforcement, rather than placing the burden on any single group.

### 2. [Reciprocal Trust and Distrust in Artificial Intelligence Systems: The Hard Problem of Regulation](http://arxiv.org/abs/2604.05826v1)
**Authors:** Martino Maggetti | **Source:** Arxiv | **Date:** 2026-04-07 | **Type:** research paper | **Relevance:** 50%

**About the authors:** Martino Maggetti is a Full Professor of Political Science at the University of Lausanne, Switzerland, promoted to full professor in 2024 after serving as associate professor since 2014. He has approximately 4,200 citations on Google Scholar and his research focuses on regulatory governance, trust in governance, and comparative public policy. He was the principal investigator and coordinator of the EU Horizon 2020 TiGRE project (Trust in Governance and Regulation in Europe, 2020-2023), a major multi-university project studying trust dynamics in European regulatory regimes. He is a former editor of the Swiss Political Science Review and a well-regarded scholar in regulatory governance, with notable prior work including 'The Principal-Agent Framework and Independent Regulatory Agencies' and the TiGRE white paper on trust and distrust in regulatory governance.

**Reliability:** MEDIUM confidence. Strengths: (1) Published in a peer-reviewed journal (AI & Society, Springer), not a preprint; (2) The author is a well-established, highly-cited political science professor with deep expertise in trust and regulatory governance; (3) The work builds on a major EU-funded research project (TiGRE). Weaknesses: (1) This is a purely conceptual paper with no empirical or technical validation; (2) The central claim about AI 'reciprocal trust' involves a potentially problematic anthropomorphization; (3) The author's expertise is in political science/regulation, not in AI/ML, limiting technical depth; (4) The paper is of limited direct relevance to mechanistic interpretability, AI alignment, or language model research specifically — it addresses AI governance from a social science perspective rather than a technical one.

**Overview:** This is a peer-reviewed article published in AI & Society (Springer, 2026) that examines the regulation of AI systems through the lens of trust theory. It argues that AI systems should be recognized as artifacts with a form of agency capable of engaging in reciprocal trust/distrust relationships with humans, and explores the regulatory implications of this framing. The paper introduces the concept of 'calibrated autonomy' as a framework for navigating these dynamics.

**Main goal:** The primary objective is to argue that trust in AI should not be viewed as a one-directional relationship (humans trusting AI), but as a reciprocal dynamic where AI systems, through their design and training, also exercise a form of trust or distrust toward humans. The paper then examines how these bidirectional trust dynamics create novel challenges ('the hard problem') for regulators tasked with overseeing AI systems.

**Key findings:**
- Finding 1: AI systems should be recognized as artifacts capable of exercising a form of agency, enabling reciprocal trust/distrust relationships with humans. This reframes the AI trust question from a unidirectional concern (can we trust AI?) to a bidirectional one (how do AI systems weight human inputs and oversight as more or less trustworthy?).
- Finding 2: The paper introduces the concept of 'calibrated autonomy' as a regulatory framework for managing the tension between granting AI systems sufficient operational independence and maintaining meaningful human oversight — drawing on the author's extensive prior work on trust in regulatory governance from the TiGRE project.
- Finding 3: These reciprocal trust dynamics create unresolved dilemmas and tensions for AI regulation and governance, complicating existing regulatory frameworks that assume human actors as the primary agents in trust relationships.

**Methodology:** This is a conceptual/theoretical paper, not an empirical study. The methodology is primarily argumentative and analytical, drawing on: (1) sociological trust theory (e.g., Sztompka), (2) prior empirical findings from the EU Horizon 2020 TiGRE project on trust in regulatory governance, (3) literature on AI agency and trustworthiness (e.g., Jacovi et al. 2021), (4) analysis of real-world regulatory frameworks including the EU AI Act, and (5) counterfactual thought experiments and analogies to historical technological disasters (e.g., Chernobyl). No new empirical data, experiments, or computational methods are presented.

**What's novel:** The novel contribution is the application of reciprocal trust theory — well-developed in political science and regulatory governance scholarship — to the AI regulation domain. Rather than treating AI trust as a one-way street (humans evaluating AI trustworthiness), the paper argues for a bidirectional framework where AI systems' own evaluative processes toward human inputs constitute a form of trust/distrust. The concept of 'calibrated autonomy' is proposed as a practical regulatory response. This bridges the gap between regulatory governance literature and the AI governance/safety conversation, which is a relatively unusual cross-disciplinary contribution.

**Limitations:** The paper is purely theoretical/conceptual, with no empirical validation of its claims about reciprocal trust dynamics in AI systems. The argument that AI systems exercise a 'form of agency' is philosophically contentious and likely to be contested by those who maintain that trust requires conscious intentionality. The paper does not engage deeply with the technical ML literature on how AI systems actually process or weight human inputs, which limits the technical specificity of its claims. The analogy between regulatory trust dynamics (involving institutional human actors) and human-AI trust dynamics may not fully hold, as the mechanisms are fundamentally different. The paper's regulatory recommendations remain at a high level of abstraction.

**Implications:** If the reciprocal trust framing is accepted, it would significantly complicate AI governance by requiring regulators to consider not just whether humans trust AI, but how AI systems are designed to evaluate and respond to human oversight. This has implications for the EU AI Act's implementation, for debates about appropriate human-in-the-loop requirements, and for the design of regulatory sandboxes. For the AI safety community specifically, the paper provides a political science perspective on how trust dynamics could affect alignment oversight mechanisms.

**Critical assessment:** The paper makes a thought-provoking conceptual argument, but its central claim — that AI systems engage in 'reciprocal trust' — risks anthropomorphizing AI behavior in misleading ways. What the paper describes as AI 'trusting' human inputs is arguably better characterized as design choices about input weighting and override permissions, not genuine trust relationships. The paper is well-grounded in the author's deep expertise in regulatory governance and trust theory, but its engagement with the technical AI/ML literature appears shallow. The references to Chernobyl and nuclear accidents as analogies seem tangential. As a conceptual piece in a social science journal, the standards of evidence are different from empirical ML papers — the paper succeeds in opening a discussion rather than proving a hypothesis. However, the claims are ambitious relative to the argumentative evidence provided. This paper will be most valuable as a bridge between political science/regulation scholars and the AI governance community, rather than as a direct contribution to mechanistic interpretability or alignment research.

**Key terms:**
- **Reciprocal Trust:** A bidirectional trust relationship where both parties in an interaction evaluate the trustworthiness of the other, here extended to argue that AI systems and humans can engage in mutual trust/distrust dynamics.
- **Calibrated Autonomy:** A proposed regulatory concept for balancing the operational independence granted to AI systems with meaningful human oversight, calibrating autonomy levels based on context and risk.
- **Regulatory Governance:** The study of how regulatory regimes — including independent agencies, rules, and oversight mechanisms — shape governance outcomes, and how trust dynamics among regulatory actors affect regime performance.
- **AI Agency:** The concept that AI systems can be recognized as exhibiting a form of agency — the capacity to act, make decisions, or exercise some degree of autonomous behavior — even if not fully equivalent to human agency.
- **Trustworthiness vs. Trust:** The distinction between the objective properties that make an entity worthy of trust (trustworthiness) and the subjective cognitive/behavioral disposition of actually trusting that entity (trust), a key conceptual distinction in trust theory.

### 3. [Brain-to-Speech: Prosody Feature Engineering and Transformer-Based Reconstruction](http://arxiv.org/abs/2604.05751v1)
**Authors:** Mohammed Salah Al-Radhi, Géza Németh, Andon Tchechmedjiev, Binbin Xu | **Source:** Arxiv | **Date:** 2026-04-07 | **Type:** preprint | **Relevance:** 50%

**About the authors:** Mohammed Salah Al-Radhi is a Research Scientist/Postdoc at Budapest University of Technology and Economics (BME), with a PhD in AI of Speech from BME (summa cum laude, 2020). He has ~209 citations on Google Scholar and ~49 publications, with his core expertise in speech synthesis, vocoders, and voice conversion; his brain-to-speech work is recent (2024-2025). Géza Németh is a Full Professor at BME's Department of Telecommunications and Media Informatics with ~981 citations and 157 publications, heading the Speech Communication and Smart Interactions Lab with decades of TTS experience. Andon Tchechmedjiev is an Associate Professor at IMT Mines Alès / EuroMov DHM with ~548 citations, specializing in computational semantics, NLP, and recently neuroengineering/BCI. Binbin Xu is affiliated with IMT Mines Alès / EuroMov DHM, working on EEG-based BCI, neural signal processing, and deep learning for neuroimaging.

**Reliability:** MEDIUM confidence. The lead author has genuine speech synthesis expertise with a solid publication record at BME, and the related MiSTR paper was accepted at Interspeech 2025, a reputable speech conference. However, concerns include: the BTS research line is very recent for these authors (starting 2024-2025); the baselines compared against are weak by current field standards; quantitative results are not specified in the abstract; the document is a non-peer-reviewed book chapter/preprint; and the language is somewhat promotional. The institutional affiliations (BME, IMT Mines Alès) are legitimate but not top-tier BCI research centers compared to groups at UCSF, Stanford, or Meta that lead this field.

**Overview:** This is an arxiv preprint (book chapter format) presenting a brain-to-speech synthesis pipeline that extracts prosodic features from intracranial EEG (iEEG) data and uses a transformer-based architecture to reconstruct speech. The central problem is improving the naturalness and intelligibility of speech reconstructed from neural signals by explicitly modeling prosodic features like intonation, pitch, and rhythm. This work is closely related to the authors' MiSTR paper accepted at Interspeech 2025.

**Main goal:** The authors aim to build a novel pipeline for extracting prosodic features from iEEG signals and use these with a custom transformer encoder architecture to generate more natural-sounding and intelligible speech from brain activity, outperforming traditional baselines like Griffin-Lim and CNN-based reconstruction.

**Key findings:**
- Finding 1: The proposed transformer-based architecture integrating prosodic features achieves superior performance over Griffin-Lim and CNN-based speech reconstruction baselines across both quantitative metrics and perceptual evaluations.
- Finding 2: A novel pipeline for extracting key prosodic features (intonation, pitch, rhythm) directly from complex iEEG brain signals is introduced and shown to enhance speech reconstruction quality.
- Finding 3: The integration of prosodic features into the transformer encoder significantly improves both intelligibility and expressiveness of reconstructed speech compared to models that do not utilize prosody information.

**Methodology:** The research uses intracranial EEG (iEEG) data with a multi-stage pipeline: (1) prosodic feature extraction from iEEG signals including intonation, pitch, and rhythm; (2) a novel transformer encoder architecture that integrates these prosodic features for mapping neural signals to speech representations (likely mel spectrograms); (3) speech waveform reconstruction from predicted spectrograms. Baselines include traditional Griffin-Lim algorithm and CNN-based reconstruction approaches. Evaluation uses both quantitative metrics (likely Pearson correlation, STOI, MCD) and perceptual/subjective metrics. The approach is described as a book chapter, suggesting it may present extended material from the authors' MiSTR framework (Interspeech 2025), which achieved a Pearson correlation of 0.91 on a public iEEG dataset.

**What's novel:** The primary novelty is the explicit integration of prosody-aware feature engineering into the brain-to-speech pipeline, specifically extracting prosodic features (pitch, intonation, rhythm) directly from iEEG signals and feeding these into a custom transformer encoder. Most prior BTS work focuses on spectral reconstruction without explicit prosody modeling. The chapter format also provides a broader tutorial-style overview of the field compared to a conference paper.

**Limitations:** The abstract does not specify dataset size, number of subjects, or the specific public iEEG dataset used, making it difficult to assess generalizability. Brain-to-speech research is inherently limited by small datasets (typically minutes of data per subject) and subject-specific electrode placements. The baselines (Griffin-Lim, CNN) are relatively weak — more competitive neural baselines from recent literature (e.g., diffusion models, wav2vec-based approaches) are not mentioned. The chapter discusses future directions (diffusion models, real-time inference) rather than implementing them, suggesting current limitations in these areas. No mention of cross-subject generalization or clinical deployment testing.

**Implications:** If validated on larger datasets, this prosody-aware approach to brain-to-speech synthesis could meaningfully advance neuroprosthetic communication devices for patients with severe speech impairments (ALS, locked-in syndrome). The explicit modeling of prosody addresses a key gap — most BCI-driven speech systems produce flat, unintelligible output. The work connects established speech synthesis expertise (vocoders, TTS) with BCI research, potentially accelerating progress in both fields.

**Critical assessment:** The paper appears to be an extended book chapter version of the authors' MiSTR framework accepted at Interspeech 2025, which reported a Pearson correlation of 0.91 and STOI of 0.73. While the results are promising, several concerns limit confidence: (1) the baselines are relatively weak (Griffin-Lim, basic CNNs) rather than state-of-the-art neural decoders from groups like Meta, Stanford, or UCSF; (2) the abstract is heavy on marketing language ('novel', 'paving the way') without specific quantitative claims; (3) as a book chapter/preprint, it has not undergone rigorous peer review beyond the related Interspeech publication; (4) the BTS field generally works with very small per-subject datasets, and generalization claims should be treated cautiously; (5) the connection between co-authors Tchechmedjiev/Xu (NLP/BCI at IMT Mines Alès) and Al-Radhi/Németh (speech synthesis at BME Budapest) suggests a cross-institutional collaboration, but the division of contributions is unclear. The authors have genuine expertise in speech synthesis and vocoding, though their BCI work is relatively recent (2024-2025).

**Key terms:**
- **iEEG (intracranial EEG):** Electroencephalography recorded from electrodes placed directly on or within the brain tissue, providing much higher spatial resolution and signal quality than scalp EEG.
- **Brain-to-Speech (BTS):** The process of decoding neural signals from brain activity and synthesizing corresponding speech audio, typically using deep learning pipelines.
- **Prosody:** The suprasegmental features of speech including intonation (pitch patterns), rhythm, stress, and tempo that convey meaning beyond individual phonemes.
- **Griffin-Lim algorithm:** An iterative signal processing algorithm for reconstructing a time-domain audio waveform from a magnitude spectrogram by estimating the phase, without using a neural vocoder.
- **Mel spectrogram:** A time-frequency representation of audio where frequencies are mapped to the mel scale (approximating human pitch perception), commonly used as an intermediate target in speech synthesis.
- **Transformer encoder:** A neural network architecture using self-attention mechanisms to capture long-range dependencies in sequential data, here adapted to map iEEG features to speech representations.

### 4. [Universal Direct Torque Controller for Permanent Magnet Synchronous Motors via Meta-Reinforcement Learning](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/ab94e18f0504585f24a8627d4be857f68bdc65f8)
