## Summary
Katja Grace argues that the AI development situation is not truly an 'arms race' in the strategically meaningful sense, even though participants describe it as one and behave accordingly. The key distinction is that the incentive structure may not actually reward speed above all else—just as playing chess aggressively doesn't make it a chess tournament if the prizes are for checkers. This framing is used to challenge justifications for moving fast at high cost to others and abandoning coordination efforts.

## Why It Matters
This argument is directly relevant to AI safety because the 'race' framing is frequently invoked to justify cutting corners on safety measures and dismissing coordination as naive. If the race framing is flawed, it undermines a major rhetorical obstacle to safety-conscious AI development.

## Related Papers
### [The Malicious Use of Artificial Intelligence: Forecasting, Prevention, and Mitigation](https://arxiv.org/abs/1802.07228) (2018)
This report by Brundage et al. examines how AI capabilities could be misused by malicious actors and discusses the dynamics of competitive AI development. It is relevant as background for understanding why race-to-the-bottom dynamics in AI development are treated as serious safety concerns.

### [Racing to the Precipice: a Model of Artificial Intelligence Development](https://www.fhi.ox.ac.uk/wp-content/uploads/Racing-to-the-precipice-a-model-of-artificial-intelligence-development.pdf) (2013)
Armstrong, Bostrom, and Shulman formally model competitive AI development as a race where developers sacrifice safety for speed, showing conditions under which coordination could prevent catastrophic outcomes. This paper is a key reference for the 'arms race' framing that Grace is critically examining.
