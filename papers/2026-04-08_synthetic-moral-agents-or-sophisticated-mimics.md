# Synthetic Moral Agents or Sophisticated Mimics?

## Overview
This is a philosophical/theoretical research paper that addresses whether AI systems (specifically LLMs) can genuinely engage in ethical reasoning or merely simulate moral thought. Rather than trying to resolve this binary question, it introduces a 'distributed moral cognition' (DMC) framework drawing on posthumanist theory, new materialism, and STS to reframe the debate around how moral reasoning emerges within human-AI assemblages rather than within AI systems alone.

## Main Goal
The paper aims to move beyond the anthropocentric framing of the AI moral agency debate (genuine vs. simulated ethics) by developing a concept of 'distributed moral cognition' (DMC) that redirects attention to the conditions under which moral reasoning emerges in human-AI systems, and to outline governance implications including relational accountability.

## Key Findings
- Finding 1: The current debate about whether AI can 'genuinely' reason morally rests on anthropocentric assumptions inherited from liberal humanist philosophy that limit productive inquiry. The paper argues this binary framing (genuine moral agent vs. sophisticated mimic) is itself the problem.
- Finding 2: The proposed DMC framework, drawing on posthumanist theory and distributed cognition, reframes the question by treating moral reasoning as an emergent property of human-AI assemblages rather than an intrinsic capacity of either humans or machines alone. This is applied to three conceptual cases: clinical ethics consultation, AI-generated moral philosophy, and autonomous vehicle decision-making.
- Finding 3: The paper proposes a model of 'relational accountability' for AI governance that moves beyond individualist blame attribution, arguing that when moral cognition is distributed across human-AI systems, responsibility must be understood relationally rather than assigned to individual actors.

## Methodology
This is a purely conceptual/philosophical paper employing theoretical argumentation rather than empirical methods. It draws on posthumanist theory (likely Hayles, Haraway, Braidotti), new materialism (likely Barad, Bennett), and science and technology studies (likely Latour, ANT). The framework is applied through conceptual case analysis of three hypothetical scenarios rather than empirical testing. No datasets, computational experiments, or quantitative evaluation metrics are used.

## What's Novel
The paper's distinctive contribution lies in synthesizing distributed cognition theory (from cognitive science) with distributed morality theory (from philosophy of technology) into a new 'distributed moral cognition' concept specifically tailored to LLM-era AI. While Heersmink (2017) explored the intersection of distributed cognition and distributed morality for artifacts generally, and Hayles (2017, 2022) developed cognitive assemblages theory, this paper specifically applies these frameworks to the question of LLM moral reasoning capabilities. The relational accountability governance model is also a potentially novel contribution.

## Limitations & Open Questions
The paper is entirely theoretical with no empirical validation of the DMC framework. The three 'conceptual cases' are likely thought experiments rather than analyzed real-world deployments, limiting their persuasive power. The framework may face the standard critique of posthumanist/new materialist approaches: by distributing agency and responsibility across assemblages, it risks diluting accountability in ways that are practically unhelpful for governance. The paper does not appear to engage with the technical details of how LLMs actually process moral scenarios, nor with empirical findings about LLM moral reasoning inconsistencies (e.g., cognitive biases, context sensitivity). The relational accountability model likely lacks the specificity needed for practical legal or regulatory implementation.

## Implications
If accepted, the DMC framework could significantly shift AI ethics discourse away from the 'genuine understanding vs. mere mimicry' debate that has dominated since the stochastic parrots paper. For AI governance, the relational accountability model could inform new regulatory approaches that distribute responsibility across developers, deployers, and users of AI systems rather than seeking to assign blame to single parties. For AI alignment research specifically, this framework suggests that alignment should be conceived not as making AI systems internally moral, but as designing human-AI assemblages that produce morally adequate outcomes.

## Critical Assessment
The paper addresses a genuine and important tension in AI ethics, but several concerns arise. First, the theoretical framework is constructed from well-established building blocks (distributed cognition, posthumanism, STS) that have been combined in similar ways before — Heersmink's 2017 paper on distributed cognition and distributed morality, Hayles' 2022 chapter on ethics for cognitive assemblages, and recent work on cognitive assemblages and AI all traverse very similar territory. The novelty of 'DMC' as a distinct concept needs to be carefully evaluated against these predecessors. Second, the paper's purely conceptual methodology limits its evidentiary weight — the conceptual cases may illustrate the framework but cannot validate it. Third, the claim that the debate 'rests on anthropocentric assumptions' is itself a common posthumanist move that some philosophers find question-begging. Fourth, without engagement with empirical LLM research showing amplified cognitive biases in moral decisions, inconsistent ethical reasoning across languages, and systematic moral mimicry patterns, the paper misses an opportunity to ground its theoretical claims. The peer-review status is unclear (sourced via OpenAlex), and the paper appears to be a preprint or early-stage publication.

## Key Terms
- **Distributed Moral Cognition (DMC):** The paper's proposed framework arguing that moral reasoning should be understood as emerging from human-AI assemblages rather than residing within individual agents, drawing on distributed cognition theory and distributed morality theory.
- **Cognitive Assemblage:** A concept from N. Katherine Hayles (2017) describing collectivities of humans, nonhumans, and computational media in which cognition, agency, and intentionality are distributed among many actors and agents.
- **New Materialism:** A philosophical movement (associated with Barad, Bennett, and others) that emphasizes the agency and significance of matter and non-human entities, challenging anthropocentric frameworks in philosophy and social theory.
- **Relational Accountability:** The paper's proposed governance model that distributes moral responsibility across participants in human-AI assemblages rather than attributing blame to individual agents.
- **Posthumanist Theory:** A theoretical perspective that challenges the centrality of the human subject in philosophy and ethics, arguing for decentered, relational understandings of agency, cognition, and moral status.

## Related Papers
### [Distributed Cognition and Distributed Morality: Agency, Artifacts and Systems](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11948-016-9802-1) (2017) — *Essential*
Heersmink explores the relationship between distributed cognition theory and distributed morality theory, examining how cognitive artifacts scaffold moral reasoning, the moral status of artifacts, and whether responsibility can be attributed to distributed systems. This paper directly establishes the intellectual foundation that the reviewed paper builds upon.
*Why relevant: The most direct foundational work — Heersmink's paper coined key terms and mapped the conceptual territory that Li's 'distributed moral cognition' framework extends to LLMs.*

### [On the Morality of Artificial Agents](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1023/B:MIND.0000035461.63578.9d) (2004) — *Essential*
Floridi and Sanders argue that there is scope for considering artificial agents as moral agents without requiring them to exhibit free will, mental states, or responsibility, using their 'Method of Abstraction' and levels of abstraction framework. A foundational paper in the AI moral agency debate that the reviewed paper seeks to move beyond.
*Why relevant: Seminal paper establishing the philosophical debate about AI moral agency that Li's paper explicitly aims to reframe through the DMC concept.*

### [Moral Mimicry: Large Language Models Produce Moral Rationalizations Tailored to Political Identity](https://aclanthology.org/2023.acl-srw.40/) (2023) — *Recommended*
Simmons demonstrates that LLMs can reproduce the moral biases associated with political groups in the US, a capability termed 'moral mimicry.' When prompted with liberal or conservative political identities, models generate text reflecting corresponding moral foundation biases, supporting the view that LLMs are sophisticated moral mimics rather than genuine moral reasoners.
*Why relevant: Provides empirical evidence directly relevant to the 'sophisticated mimics' characterization in Li's title, and represents the kind of empirical work the reviewed paper could have engaged with more substantively.*

### [Ethics for Cognitive Assemblages: Who's in Charge Here?](https://link.springer.com/10.1007/978-3-031-04958-3_11) (2022) — *Essential*
Hayles sketches ethical frameworks for cognitive assemblages — collectivities of humans, nonhumans, and computational media where cognition and agency are distributed. She examines algorithmic responsibility, harms specific to assemblages, and whether computational agents deserve ethical consideration. Published in the Palgrave Handbook of Critical Posthumanism.
*Why relevant: Hayles' chapter on ethics for cognitive assemblages directly parallels Li's project of developing ethical/governance frameworks for distributed human-AI moral reasoning, making it a key predecessor and potential overlap.*
