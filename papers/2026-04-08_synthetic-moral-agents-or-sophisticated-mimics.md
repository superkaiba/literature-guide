## Summary
This paper challenges the binary debate of whether LLMs are 'genuine' moral reasoners or mere mimics, arguing the framing is too anthropocentric. It introduces 'distributed moral cognition' (DMC), a framework drawn from posthumanist theory and STS, to redirect inquiry toward how moral reasoning emerges within human-AI assemblages. The framework is applied to clinical ethics, AI-generated philosophy, and autonomous vehicle ethics, concluding with a 'relational accountability' governance model.

## Why It Matters
For researchers working on AI alignment and language model personas, this paper offers a non-anthropocentric lens for evaluating moral behavior in LLMs, which is directly relevant to questions about whether aligning AI to human values requires inner moral understanding or can be achieved through structured relational contexts.

## Related Papers
### [Moral Crumple Zones: Cautionary Tales in Human-Robot Interaction](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2757729) (2016)
This paper by Madeleine Clare Elish introduces the concept of 'moral crumple zones,' arguing that in human-robot systems, moral responsibility tends to collapse onto the human operator even when the system bears causal responsibility. It is highly relevant to relational accountability frameworks and how blame is distributed in sociotechnical assemblages.

### [Aligning AI With Shared Human Values](https://arxiv.org/abs/2008.02275) (2020)
This paper by Hendrycks et al. introduces the ETHICS dataset to benchmark whether language models can learn human moral judgments across frameworks like justice, virtue, and deontology. It empirically probes the 'mimic vs. reasoner' question and is a key empirical counterpart to the philosophical framing in Li's paper.
