## Summary
This paper argues that AI evaluation has been epistemologically constrained by Turing's behavioral paradigm for seven decades, preventing researchers from asking questions about internal processes and mechanisms. Drawing a structural parallel to psychology's behaviorist-to-cognitivist transition, the author contends that AI needs a similar 'cognitive revolution' that supplements behavioral evidence with process-level, mechanistic evidence to properly attribute intelligence and distinguish systems achieving identical outputs through fundamentally different computational means.

## Why It Matters
This directly motivates the mechanistic interpretability research agenda by providing a philosophical grounding for why behavioral benchmarks are insufficient for AI alignment and safety—understanding internal computational processes is not merely scientifically interesting but epistemologically necessary for making reliable intelligence and capability attributions.

## Related Papers
### [Shortcut Learning in Deep Neural Networks](https://www.nature.com/articles/s42256-020-00257-z) (2020)
This paper empirically demonstrates that deep neural networks frequently achieve high benchmark performance by exploiting spurious statistical correlations ('shortcuts') rather than learning the intended underlying task. It provides concrete evidence for the paper's central claim that identical behavioral outputs can arise from fundamentally different and potentially unreliable internal computational processes.

### [Language Models Don't Always Say What They Think: Unfaithful Explanations in Chain-of-Thought Prompting](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.04388) (2023)
This paper shows that chain-of-thought explanations generated by LLMs are often unfaithful to the actual computations underlying their outputs, meaning behavioral and verbal evidence can be systematically misleading about internal processes. This directly supports the argument that behavioral evidence alone is insufficient for understanding AI systems and motivates mechanistic investigation.
