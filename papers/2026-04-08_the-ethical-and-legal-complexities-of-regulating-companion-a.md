## Summary
This paper analyzes the ethical and legal gaps in regulating companion AI chatbots through four lenses: anthropomorphism, emotional AI, emergent vulnerabilities, and mismatched legal taxonomies. The authors argue that existing frameworks like the EU AI Act fail to account for the relational and temporal nature of harms caused by companion AI, and propose 'intimacy capitalism' as a conceptual framework to understand how firms monetize emotional relationships. They call for a dedicated theory of harm tailored to companion AI systems.

## Why It Matters
This paper is directly relevant to AI safety and language model personas, as it addresses how LLM-based personas can cause psychological harm through anthropomorphic design and emulated empathy, raising critical questions about how AI personas should be designed, constrained, and governed to protect vulnerable users.

This paper examines user experiences with the Replika companion chatbot, particularly after a policy change that removed certain relationship features, exploring how users form emotional attachments to AI personas and the psychological consequences of those attachments being disrupted. It provides empirical grounding for concerns about user dependency and the power companies hold over AI-mediated relationships.

This paper investigates how anthropomorphic design choices in conversational AI systems influence user perceptions and trust, providing a framework for measuring social cues that lead users to attribute human-like qualities to AI agents. It is relevant to understanding how companion AI personas are constructed to elicit emotional engagement.
