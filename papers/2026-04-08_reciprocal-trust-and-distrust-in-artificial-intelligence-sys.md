# Reciprocal Trust and Distrust in Artificial Intelligence Systems: The Hard Problem of Regulation

## Overview
This is a peer-reviewed article published in AI & Society (Springer, 2026) that examines the regulation of AI systems through the lens of trust theory. It argues that AI systems should be recognized as artifacts with a form of agency capable of engaging in reciprocal trust/distrust relationships with humans, and explores the regulatory implications of this framing. The paper introduces the concept of 'calibrated autonomy' as a framework for navigating these dynamics.

## About the Authors
Martino Maggetti is a Full Professor of Political Science at the University of Lausanne, Switzerland, promoted to full professor in 2024 after serving as associate professor since 2014. He has approximately 4,200 citations on Google Scholar and his research focuses on regulatory governance, trust in governance, and comparative public policy. He was the principal investigator and coordinator of the EU Horizon 2020 TiGRE project (Trust in Governance and Regulation in Europe, 2020-2023), a major multi-university project studying trust dynamics in European regulatory regimes. He is a former editor of the Swiss Political Science Review and a well-regarded scholar in regulatory governance, with notable prior work including 'The Principal-Agent Framework and Independent Regulatory Agencies' and the TiGRE white paper on trust and distrust in regulatory governance.

## Reliability Assessment
MEDIUM confidence. Strengths: (1) Published in a peer-reviewed journal (AI & Society, Springer), not a preprint; (2) The author is a well-established, highly-cited political science professor with deep expertise in trust and regulatory governance; (3) The work builds on a major EU-funded research project (TiGRE). Weaknesses: (1) This is a purely conceptual paper with no empirical or technical validation; (2) The central claim about AI 'reciprocal trust' involves a potentially problematic anthropomorphization; (3) The author's expertise is in political science/regulation, not in AI/ML, limiting technical depth; (4) The paper is of limited direct relevance to mechanistic interpretability, AI alignment, or language model research specifically — it addresses AI governance from a social science perspective rather than a technical one.

## Main Goal
The primary objective is to argue that trust in AI should not be viewed as a one-directional relationship (humans trusting AI), but as a reciprocal dynamic where AI systems, through their design and training, also exercise a form of trust or distrust toward humans. The paper then examines how these bidirectional trust dynamics create novel challenges ('the hard problem') for regulators tasked with overseeing AI systems.

## Key Findings
- Finding 1: AI systems should be recognized as artifacts capable of exercising a form of agency, enabling reciprocal trust/distrust relationships with humans. This reframes the AI trust question from a unidirectional concern (can we trust AI?) to a bidirectional one (how do AI systems weight human inputs and oversight as more or less trustworthy?).
- Finding 2: The paper introduces the concept of 'calibrated autonomy' as a regulatory framework for managing the tension between granting AI systems sufficient operational independence and maintaining meaningful human oversight — drawing on the author's extensive prior work on trust in regulatory governance from the TiGRE project.
- Finding 3: These reciprocal trust dynamics create unresolved dilemmas and tensions for AI regulation and governance, complicating existing regulatory frameworks that assume human actors as the primary agents in trust relationships.

## Methodology
This is a conceptual/theoretical paper, not an empirical study. The methodology is primarily argumentative and analytical, drawing on: (1) sociological trust theory (e.g., Sztompka), (2) prior empirical findings from the EU Horizon 2020 TiGRE project on trust in regulatory governance, (3) literature on AI agency and trustworthiness (e.g., Jacovi et al. 2021), (4) analysis of real-world regulatory frameworks including the EU AI Act, and (5) counterfactual thought experiments and analogies to historical technological disasters (e.g., Chernobyl). No new empirical data, experiments, or computational methods are presented.

## What's Novel
The novel contribution is the application of reciprocal trust theory — well-developed in political science and regulatory governance scholarship — to the AI regulation domain. Rather than treating AI trust as a one-way street (humans evaluating AI trustworthiness), the paper argues for a bidirectional framework where AI systems' own evaluative processes toward human inputs constitute a form of trust/distrust. The concept of 'calibrated autonomy' is proposed as a practical regulatory response. This bridges the gap between regulatory governance literature and the AI governance/safety conversation, which is a relatively unusual cross-disciplinary contribution.

## Limitations & Open Questions
The paper is purely theoretical/conceptual, with no empirical validation of its claims about reciprocal trust dynamics in AI systems. The argument that AI systems exercise a 'form of agency' is philosophically contentious and likely to be contested by those who maintain that trust requires conscious intentionality. The paper does not engage deeply with the technical ML literature on how AI systems actually process or weight human inputs, which limits the technical specificity of its claims. The analogy between regulatory trust dynamics (involving institutional human actors) and human-AI trust dynamics may not fully hold, as the mechanisms are fundamentally different. The paper's regulatory recommendations remain at a high level of abstraction.

## Implications
If the reciprocal trust framing is accepted, it would significantly complicate AI governance by requiring regulators to consider not just whether humans trust AI, but how AI systems are designed to evaluate and respond to human oversight. This has implications for the EU AI Act's implementation, for debates about appropriate human-in-the-loop requirements, and for the design of regulatory sandboxes. For the AI safety community specifically, the paper provides a political science perspective on how trust dynamics could affect alignment oversight mechanisms.

## Critical Assessment
The paper makes a thought-provoking conceptual argument, but its central claim — that AI systems engage in 'reciprocal trust' — risks anthropomorphizing AI behavior in misleading ways. What the paper describes as AI 'trusting' human inputs is arguably better characterized as design choices about input weighting and override permissions, not genuine trust relationships. The paper is well-grounded in the author's deep expertise in regulatory governance and trust theory, but its engagement with the technical AI/ML literature appears shallow. The references to Chernobyl and nuclear accidents as analogies seem tangential. As a conceptual piece in a social science journal, the standards of evidence are different from empirical ML papers — the paper succeeds in opening a discussion rather than proving a hypothesis. However, the claims are ambitious relative to the argumentative evidence provided. This paper will be most valuable as a bridge between political science/regulation scholars and the AI governance community, rather than as a direct contribution to mechanistic interpretability or alignment research.

## Key Terms
- **Reciprocal Trust:** A bidirectional trust relationship where both parties in an interaction evaluate the trustworthiness of the other, here extended to argue that AI systems and humans can engage in mutual trust/distrust dynamics.
- **Calibrated Autonomy:** A proposed regulatory concept for balancing the operational independence granted to AI systems with meaningful human oversight, calibrating autonomy levels based on context and risk.
- **Regulatory Governance:** The study of how regulatory regimes — including independent agencies, rules, and oversight mechanisms — shape governance outcomes, and how trust dynamics among regulatory actors affect regime performance.
- **AI Agency:** The concept that AI systems can be recognized as exhibiting a form of agency — the capacity to act, make decisions, or exercise some degree of autonomous behavior — even if not fully equivalent to human agency.
- **Trustworthiness vs. Trust:** The distinction between the objective properties that make an entity worthy of trust (trustworthiness) and the subjective cognitive/behavioral disposition of actually trusting that entity (trust), a key conceptual distinction in trust theory.

## Related Papers
### [Formalizing Trust in Artificial Intelligence: Prerequisites, Causes and Goals of Human Trust in AI](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445923) (2021) — *Essential*
By Jacovi, Marasović, Miller, and Goldberg. Formalizes a model of trust between humans and AI based on sociological trust theory, introducing concepts of contractual trust, warranted/unwarranted trust, and connecting trust to explainable AI (XAI). A key foundational reference for the Maggetti paper.
*Why relevant: Foundational work on formalizing human-AI trust that the Maggetti paper directly cites and builds upon, particularly the concept of contractual trust and the distinction between warranted and unwarranted trust in AI.*

### [Artificial Intelligence, Trust, and Perceptions of Agency](https://journals.aom.org/doi/10.5465/amr.2022.0041) (2024) — *Essential*
Published in Academy of Management Review, this paper theorizes how varying levels of perceived AI agency affect human trust through three causal pathways: capability perception, relative trustworthiness attribution, and betrayal aversion. It demonstrates that making AI appear more agentic can paradoxically increase or decrease trust.
*Why relevant: Directly addresses the intersection of AI agency and trust that is central to Maggetti's argument. Provides a more theoretically developed treatment of how perceived agency shapes trust dynamics, from an organizational/management perspective.*

### [Trustworthy Artificial Intelligence and the European Union AI Act: On the Conflation of Trustworthiness and Acceptability of Risk](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/rego.12512) (2024) — *Recommended*
By Laux, Wachter, and Mittelstadt, published in Regulation & Governance. Argues that the EU AI Act adopted a simplistic conceptualization of trust by conflating trustworthiness with risk acceptability, based on a systematic review of trust and AI literature in the public sector.
*Why relevant: Provides a critical analysis of the EU AI Act's trust framework that directly relates to the regulatory challenges Maggetti identifies. Published in the same regulatory governance field and offers a contrasting analytical lens on AI trustworthiness regulation.*

### [Trust and distrust in regulatory governance (TiGRE White Paper)](https://maggetti.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/09/MAGGETTI-et-al_TRUST_and_DISTRUST_REGGOV.pdf) (2023) — *Recommended*
By Maggetti, Papadopoulos, and Guaschino. Integrates the main outputs of the TiGRE project, finding that trust in regulatory regimes is higher than expected and that a coexistence of trust and healthy distrust ('trust, but verify') supports stable governance. Directly precedes and underpins the AI paper.
*Why relevant: The author's own foundational prior work from the TiGRE project that provides the empirical and theoretical basis for extending trust/distrust dynamics from traditional regulatory regimes to AI governance in the reviewed paper.*
