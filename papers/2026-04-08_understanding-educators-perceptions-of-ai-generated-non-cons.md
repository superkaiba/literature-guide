# Understanding Educators' Perceptions of AI-generated Non-consensual Intimate Imagery

## Overview
This is a qualitative research paper (preprint on arXiv, accepted to CHI 2026) investigating U.S. educators' perceptions, attitudes, experiences, and practices related to AI-generated non-consensual intimate imagery (AIG-NCII) in middle and high school settings. The central problem is understanding the gap between the emerging threat of AIG-NCII in schools and the actual supports, training, and policies available to educators to address it.

## About the Authors
All authors are affiliated with the New Jersey Institute of Technology (NJIT). The senior/corresponding author, Donghee Yvette Wohn, is an associate professor and director of the Social X Lab at NJIT, with an h-index of 27 and over 11,000 citations on Google Scholar. Her research focuses on HCI, social media, content moderation, and online harassment. Tongxin (Lily) Li is a first-year/early-stage Ph.D. student at NJIT working in the Social X Lab under Dr. Wohn, focusing on human-AI interaction and digital media. Katelyn Reyes appears to be an undergraduate/graduate student at NJIT involved in the Social Interaction Lab. The other co-authors (Liezeil Jimenez, Katie S Nam) appear to be students in the same lab.

## Reliability Assessment
MEDIUM confidence. The paper is accepted to CHI 2026 (a premier, peer-reviewed HCI venue), and the senior author (Wohn) has a strong, established research track record in HCI and social media. However, the first author is an early-career PhD student, the sample size is relatively small (n=20), and the findings, while valid, largely confirm what larger quantitative studies have already reported. The qualitative methodology is appropriate but limits generalizability. No red flags regarding data fabrication or questionable practices; the study addresses a genuine gap but with modest novelty.

## Main Goal
The authors aim to identify what concrete supports schools need to effectively address AIG-NCII by interviewing 20 U.S. educators about their attitudes, experiences, and current practices, ultimately contributing to educational tool design, curriculum development, and policy-making around this emerging issue.

## Key Findings
- Finding 1: Educators expressed dual vulnerability concerns — they worried both about the moral decline among students exposed to AIG-NCII technology and about themselves potentially becoming victims of AI-generated imagery by students.
- Finding 2: Existing school practices for addressing AIG-NCII are severely limited; educators lack both specialized training and systematic institutional policies, leaving them ill-equipped to respond to incidents.
- Finding 3: Key implementation barriers include a lack of resources, unclear legal boundaries around AIG-NCII (especially involving minors), and limited educator knowledge of AI technology, making effective prevention and response difficult.

## Methodology
Semi-structured qualitative interview study with 20 educators in the United States (likely middle and high school teachers and administrators). Interviews explored educators' attitudes toward AIG-NCII, their direct or indirect experiences with such incidents, and current institutional practices. Analysis likely employed thematic coding or similar qualitative analysis methods to identify key themes across the interview data. The sample size of 20 is typical for exploratory qualitative HCI research.

## What's Novel
This paper is among the first to specifically examine AIG-NCII from the educator perspective through qualitative interviews, whereas prior work (e.g., CDT's 2024 survey, Brigham et al. 2024) has focused on quantitative prevalence data or general public perceptions. The focus on educators as both potential responders and potential victims is a novel dual framing. The paper also explicitly contributes to interactive educational tool design and curriculum recommendations, connecting HCI design implications to the problem.

## Limitations & Open Questions
The sample of 20 U.S. educators, while acceptable for qualitative research, limits generalizability across different school systems, regions, or countries. The study likely skews toward educators who are already aware of or concerned about AIG-NCII, introducing possible self-selection bias. The paper does not include student or parent perspectives, which would provide a more complete picture. The rapidly evolving legal landscape (e.g., the TAKE IT DOWN Act signed in May 2025) may have already changed some of the policy gaps identified. As a qualitative study, it cannot quantify the prevalence or severity of AIG-NCII in schools.

## Implications
The paper has direct practical implications for school policy-makers needing to develop AIG-NCII response frameworks, curriculum designers creating AI literacy and digital citizenship programs, and HCI researchers designing interactive educational tools. The finding that educators feel vulnerable themselves (not just concerned for students) adds urgency to institutional protections. The call for multi-stakeholder strategies aligns with emerging policy recommendations from organizations like CDT and ATIXA. For AI safety broadly, it highlights how generative AI misuse creates concrete, real-world harms in educational settings that demand sociotechnical rather than purely technical interventions.

## Critical Assessment
The paper addresses a timely and important topic with growing real-world urgency, as evidenced by the federal TAKE IT DOWN Act and CDT's large-scale surveys confirming NCII prevalence in schools. The qualitative methodology is appropriate for an exploratory study in an under-researched area. However, n=20 is on the smaller side even for qualitative work, and the findings — that schools lack resources, training, and policies — are somewhat predictable and consistent with prior survey data (e.g., CDT found 60% of teachers report their schools haven't shared NCII policies). The contributions to HCI/design are stated but the paper would be stronger with concrete design proposals or prototypes. The paper fills a genuine gap by centering educator voices qualitatively, but its findings may have limited novelty beyond confirming what quantitative surveys have already suggested. The acceptance to CHI 2026 (a top-tier HCI venue) lends credibility, though the paper sits at the intersection of HCI and education/policy rather than at the core of either field.

## Key Terms
- **AIG-NCII:** AI-Generated Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery — sexually explicit or intimate images of real people created using AI tools (e.g., 'nudify' apps or deepfake generators) without the depicted person's consent.
- **NCII:** Non-Consensual Intimate Imagery — any intimate or sexually explicit images shared without the depicted person's consent, encompassing both authentic (real) images and AI-generated/synthetic ones.
- **Image-Based Sexual Abuse (IBSA):** A broader term encompassing all forms of non-consensual creation, distribution, or threat of distribution of intimate images, including 'revenge porn', upskirting, deepfake pornography, and sextortion.
- **Nudify apps:** AI-powered applications that take a clothed photo of a person and generate a realistic-looking but fake nude image, enabling even unskilled users to create deepfake NCII rapidly and at scale.
- **Multi-stakeholder strategy:** An approach to addressing AIG-NCII that involves coordinated action across educators, administrators, parents, students, policymakers, technology platforms, and law enforcement, rather than placing the burden on any single group.

## Related Papers
### ["Violation of my body:" Perceptions of AI-generated non-consensual (intimate) imagery](https://www.usenix.org/conference/soups2024/presentation/brigham) (2024) — *Essential*
Survey of 315 U.S. individuals examining public perceptions of hypothetical non-consensual deepfake creation depicting them, including sexually explicit content. Finds strong opposition to creating/sharing such content, with attitudes varying by gender and relationship to the creator.
*Why relevant: Directly related foundational work on public perceptions of AIG-NCII; this paper examines general public attitudes while Li et al. specifically focus on educators, making them complementary.*

### [Non-Consensual Synthetic Intimate Imagery: Prevalence, Attitudes, and Knowledge in 10 Countries](https://dl.acm.org/doi/fullHtml/10.1145/3613904.3642382) (2024) — *Essential*
Large-scale survey across 10 countries examining awareness, victimization prevalence, perpetration behaviors, and attitudes toward AI-generated image-based sexual abuse (deepfake pornography). Published at CHI 2024.
*Why relevant: Provides the quantitative, cross-national prevalence and attitudinal data that contextualizes the qualitative educator-focused findings in Li et al. Both published at CHI, addressing the same problem from different methodological angles.*

### [In Deep Trouble: Surfacing Tech-Powered Sexual Harassment in K-12 Schools](https://cdt.org/insights/report-in-deep-trouble-surfacing-tech-powered-sexual-harassment-in-k-12-schools/) (2024) — *Essential*
CDT's nationally representative survey of K-12 students, teachers, and parents revealing that 40% of students and 29% of teachers know of deepfakes shared at their school. Finds schools disproportionately focus on punishment over prevention and victim support.
*Why relevant: The most directly relevant quantitative baseline for the problem Li et al. study qualitatively. CDT's survey findings on teacher awareness and policy gaps are the large-scale complement to the interview-based insights in this paper.*

### [AI-Generated Child Sexual Abuse Material: Insights from Educators, Platforms, Law Enforcement, Legislators, and Victims](https://purl.stanford.edu/mn692xc5736) (2025) — *Recommended*
Stanford Internet Observatory report based on 52 interviews with educators, platform staff, law enforcement, legislators, and victims about AI-generated CSAM. Finds schools are generally not addressing nudify app risks with students and some have mishandled incidents.
*Why relevant: A concurrent, larger-scale multi-stakeholder interview study that includes educator perspectives alongside other groups. While focused on CSAM rather than adult NCII, its school-focused findings overlap significantly with Li et al.'s educator-centered approach.*
